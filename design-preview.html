Can consciousness exist without subjective experience? Or is subjectivity just what consciousness is?
✦

This is essentially the hard problem of consciousness — the explanatory gap between physical processes and qualia. Chalmers argues these are irreducible: you can explain every functional behavior of the brain without ever explaining why there is something it's like to see red.

Dennett takes the opposite view. He thinks phenomenal consciousness is a kind of user illusion — the brain's narrative about itself. There's no "Cartesian theatre" where experience happens; just recursive self-modeling that creates the impression of inner life.

Most physicalists try to thread this needle: consciousness is real, but it's identical to (not separate from) certain brain states. The challenge is explaining the identity, not just asserting it.

But if Dennett's right and it's all functional — doesn't that mean a philosophical zombie is logically possible?

Qualia Qualia are the intrinsic, subjective qualities of experience — the "redness" of red, the painfulness of pain. They're often described as ineffable (impossible to fully communicate), intrinsic (not about relations), private, and directly apprehensible. Block distinguishes phenomenal consciousness (what it's like) from access consciousness (availability to reasoning and report).
✦

That's exactly the zombie argument — and it's the sharpest challenge to functionalism. If a being can be physically and functionally identical to a human but lack inner experience, then consciousness can't be just functional organization. Chalmers uses this to argue for property dualism: experience is a fundamental feature of the world, not reducible to physics.

Dennett's counter: zombies aren't actually conceivable once you work through the details. A functional duplicate would behave as though it had qualia, report having them, be moved by them — at what point is the "experience" genuinely absent vs. just philosophically stipulated away?

What makes phenomenal consciousness different from access consciousness?

Phenomenal consciousness is the "what it's like" dimension — raw subjective feel. Access consciousness is about a mental state being globally available for reasoning, verbal report, and action control.


The split matters because in theory you could have one without the other. A state could be access-conscious (reportable, usable) but phenomenally absent — which is what Dennett suspects is actually the case for most "inner experience."


Ned Block coined the distinction to clarify debates: when people argue about consciousness, they're often talking past each other because they mean different things.
